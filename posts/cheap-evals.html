Cheap evals that actually catch regressions

2026·06·25 — 5 min

Most of the value I get from a model in production comes from a boring place: a small evaluation suite that runs in seconds and tells me, honestly, whether I just made things worse. This is the setup I keep coming back to, and why each piece earns its place.

Three things a good eval has to do

Before any harness, I write down what I'm actually trying to measure. For most of my work it comes down to three properties — in priority order:

  • Catch regressions. If quality drops, the number has to move. Everything else is secondary.
  • Run cheaply. If it costs real money or minutes, I won't run it on every change — and an eval you don't run is just a file.
  • Stay stable. The same input should give roughly the same score tomorrow, or I can't trust a delta.

That last one is the quiet killer. An LLM-judged metric that swings ±10 points between runs can't tell you whether a 0.02 change is signal or noise.

The harness

The whole thing is small enough to read in one sitting. Score each example, average, compare to the baseline on disk:

def evaluate(model, dataset, baseline):
    scores = [grade(model(ex.input), ex.gold) for ex in dataset]
    mean = sum(scores) / len(scores)
    delta = mean - baseline.mean
    if delta < -baseline.tolerance:
        raise Regression(f"quality dropped {delta:.3f}")
    return mean, delta
If a regression can't fail your build, it isn't an eval — it's a dashboard you'll stop looking at.

On graders

For anything fuzzy I use a stronger model as a tiered grader: a cheap pass for the obvious cases and an expensive pass only for the disagreements. It keeps the average cost near the cheap grader while keeping the accuracy near the expensive one.

What I deliberately skip

Restraint is most of the trick. Things I've learned to leave out, at least early:

  1. Giant benchmark suites that take an hour — they don't fit the "run on every change" loop.
  2. Metrics nobody can interpret. If I can't explain what moved the number, I can't act on it.
  3. Anything that needs a GPU just to grade. The grader should be cheaper than the thing it grades.

None of this is fancy. But a fast, stable, regression-catching eval is the difference between shipping with confidence and shipping on vibes — and I've done enough of the second to prefer the first. More on the dataset side of this in a future post.
